Profile

How I learned to break things

For a year and a half I taught web development. That meant reading other people's code every single day — dozens of student projects, each broken in its own creative way. I had to find the defect, understand the root cause, and explain it to someone who didn't yet think like a developer.

It turned out to be the best testing training I could have asked for. You stop assuming software works the way it was meant to. You start looking at it the way the person on the other end actually experiences it.

Since then I've spent years at Concentrix validating data workflows and enterprise reporting against expected outcomes — working in Salesforce to extract and validate data, sitting close to customer service teams, and learning how case management actually behaves day to day. I know what it looks like when a system technically passes but still fails the user. And I know how to document it clearly enough that it gets fixed.

01 — User perspective
I test as the end user
Years on technical helpdesks and beside service teams taught me what real users hit — not what the spec assumed they would.
02 — Documentation
Defects anyone can reproduce
Teaching forced me to explain problems precisely. That habit carries into every defect report I write in JIRA.
03 — Technical depth

A full-stack and SQL background means I can trace why something broke, not just flag that it did.
